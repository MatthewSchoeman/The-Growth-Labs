Ship it.
Then do it again.

Writing code is a fraction of the job. Real software is planned, designed, built, reviewed, tested, deployed, and maintained — usually by a team, and never just once. The software development lifecycle (SDLC) is the repeatable process that carries an idea all the way to production and keeps it healthy there. This course walks the whole loop: requirements and design, version control and code review, CI/CD and deployment strategies, monitoring and maintenance — the difference between code that runs on your machine and software that ships, over and over, without breaking. We'll ship Snip, the app from the capstone, through every phase.

BeginnerModule 00

What the SDLC is

Here's the thing every course before this one quietly assumed: that you already knew what to build, that you were working alone, and that the job ended when the code ran. In real software, none of that holds. The software development lifecycle is the process that turns a rough idea into working, maintained software — through a sequence of phases that most teams recognize: plan what to build, design how, build it, test it, review it, deploy it, and operate it in production — then feed what you learn back into the next round.

Writing code sits in the middle of that list, and it's a smaller slice than newcomers expect. Understanding the problem, agreeing on scope, coordinating with other people, keeping the thing running after launch — that's the bulk of professional software work. A process exists because it makes the outcome predictable: it catches problems early, keeps a team moving in the same direction, and turns "it works on my machine" into "it ships reliably, again and again."

The single most important shift in how this is done: the lifecycle is a loop, not a line. The old model ran the phases once, start to finish. Modern teams run them continuously in small cycles, shipping a little, learning, and going around again — which is the through-line of this entire course.

Software development means writing code.
Coding is one phase. The lifecycle also covers figuring out what to build, designing it, reviewing it, releasing it safely, and maintaining it for years. Most of the effort — and most of the cost — lives outside the editor.
Process is bureaucracy that slows developers down.
Good process removes friction, not adds it. It's what lets ten people change the same codebase without chaos, and what turns a scary release into a routine one. The alternative to process isn't speed — it's rework and outages.
ModelsBeginnerModule 01

Waterfall vs iterative

The original lifecycle model is Waterfall: run the phases once, in strict order, finishing each before the next begins. Gather all the requirements, then design everything, then build everything, then test, then release. Its appeal is real — it's clear, documented, and easy to plan on a chart. Its problem is just as real: you don't find out whether you built the right thing until the very end, and by then a single changed requirement can invalidate months of work.

The iterative (and incremental) model runs the phases in small, repeating cycles instead. Each iteration produces a working slice, gets feedback, and adjusts. Change stops being a catastrophe and becomes just the input to the next cycle. Watch what a late requirement change does to each approach:

▸ Waterfall
↻ Iterative
In Waterfall a late change ripples backward through finished phases. Iterating, the same change is just the next cycle's work.
Waterfall is simply wrong — always iterate.
Waterfall fits stable, well-understood work where requirements genuinely won't move — some hardware, safety-critical, or fixed-contract projects. The trouble is that most software lives in uncertainty, where iterating beats guessing everything up front.
More up-front planning always lowers risk.
In an uncertain domain, detailed up-front plans lock in decisions before you've learned enough to make them — raising risk. Iterating buys information: you plan a little, build a little, and let real feedback correct the course.
AgileBeginnerModule 02

Agile & its values

Iterating became a movement in 2001 when a group of practitioners wrote the Agile Manifesto — a short statement of four value preferences. Put in plain terms: favour people and collaboration over rigid process and tooling; favour working software over exhaustive documentation; favour working with the customer over negotiating contracts; and favour responding to change over rigidly following a plan. The word to notice is favour — the things on the right still have value; Agile just weights the left more heavily.

Underneath sit principles that all point the same way: deliver working software frequently in small increments, welcome changing requirements even late, keep business and developers talking daily, and reflect regularly on how to improve. Agile isn't a specific tool or a rule about standups — it's a bias toward short feedback loops. The concrete frameworks people use to practise it, chiefly Scrum and Kanban, are the subject of the final module.

Agile in one sentence

Build software in small slices, show it to real users often, and let what you learn steer what you build next — instead of committing to a full plan before you've written a line. Every practice in the rest of this course, from short-lived branches to continuous deployment, exists to make that feedback loop faster.

Agile means no documentation and no planning.
It means just enough of both, valued below working software and adaptability. Agile teams still plan and document — they just don't let a plan or a spec become more important than delivering something real and responding to feedback.
Agile is just having a daily standup.
Ceremonies are the visible surface, not the substance. A team can run every Scrum ritual and still be un-agile if it can't ship small and adapt. The point is the short feedback loop; the meetings only matter insofar as they serve it.
RequirementsBeginnerModule 03

Requirements

Before anyone writes code, someone has to decide what to build — and getting that wrong is the most expensive mistake in the lifecycle, because every later phase amplifies it. Agile teams capture requirements as user stories: short, plain-language descriptions of a need from a user's point of view, in the form "As a <role>, I want <goal>, so that <reason>." The format forces you to name who wants it and, crucially, why.

A story isn't done until it has acceptance criteria — concrete conditions that decide when it's finished. Build a story for a new Snip feature and watch its criteria and quality checks fill in:

Requirements means a giant specification document.
A thick spec written up front goes stale the moment things change. Small user stories plus a conversation capture intent without pretending to predict everything — and they're easy to reprioritize as you learn.
Build exactly what the ticket literally says.
The so that clause is the real requirement. Understanding the underlying need lets you spot a simpler solution — or notice the request wouldn't actually solve the user's problem. Literal-mindedness ships the wrong feature correctly.
DesignIntermediateModule 04

Design & architecture

With a story agreed, the next question is how. Design is where you decide the shape before committing to the details: which components change, what the interfaces look like, how data flows, and which trade-offs you're accepting. For anything non-trivial, teams write it down — a short design doc or architecture decision record (ADR) — so the reasoning is reviewable before code makes it expensive to revisit.

A good design doc is short and focused on decisions, not ceremony:

# ADR: Custom aliases for Snip
Context   Users want memorable codes (snip.ly/launch) instead of 4C92.
Decision  Add an optional alias on POST /shorten; store in links.code;
          reuse the existing UNIQUE index to reject duplicates.
Options   (a) separate aliases table  (b) reuse code column ✓
Consequences  Sequential + custom codes share one column and one
          uniqueness guarantee; no new join on the redirect hot path.

Notice this design leans entirely on ideas from earlier courses — which is the point. Architecture isn't invented fresh each time; it's the accumulated judgement those courses were teaching.

↳ Design Patterns · OOP · EF · SQL
Right-sizing the design draws on all of them: the patterns and SOLID from Design Patterns and OOP, the schema and index thinking from SQL and EF Core, and the layered architecture from the Capstone. Design is where that technical knowledge is spent deliberately, before the code locks it in.
Design means big diagrams for everything up front.
Match the design effort to the risk. A one-line change needs none; a new subsystem deserves an ADR. Over-designing is its own waste — some structure is best left to emerge as the code teaches you what it needs.
Skip design — just start coding and figure it out.
A little design catches the expensive mistakes (the wrong data model, an interface that leaks) while they're still cheap words on a page. The skill is finding the middle: enough design to de-risk, not so much that it's fiction.
Version ControlBeginnerModule 05

Version control

Now the building — but building with other people needs a way to change the same code without stepping on each other. That's version control, and in practice it means Git. The core flow: keep the shared history on main, branch off to do a piece of work in isolation, make small focused commits, then open a pull request to merge it back after review. main stays releasable at all times.

Step through the life of a feature branch for Snip's custom-alias work:

main feature branch merge
Short-lived branches and small commits keep merges painless and main always shippable.
↳ Every course before this
All the code you wrote — from the Big O exercises to Snip — belongs in a repository like this. Version control is the substrate the whole lifecycle runs on: it's how review happens (Module 06), what CI builds (Module 08), and what you roll back to when a deploy goes wrong (Module 09).
Commit everything at the end in one big commit.
Small, focused commits with clear messages make history readable, reviews tractable, and bugs bisectable. One giant "did stuff" commit throws away Git's biggest advantage — the ability to see and undo change in meaningful units.
Long-lived branches are fine; merge when it's all done.
The longer a branch lives, the further it drifts from main, and the uglier the eventual merge. Short-lived branches — or trunk-based development with feature flags — keep integration continuous and conflicts small, which is what CI/CD assumes.
ReviewIntermediateModule 06

Code review

A pull request usually can't merge until another person has read it. Code review does more than catch bugs: it spreads knowledge across the team, keeps the codebase consistent, and makes ownership collective — the reviewer now understands the change too. Good reviewers look past "does it work" to design, readability, edge cases, and whether the tests actually cover the behavior.

Walk a Snip pull request through review, from opened to merged:

open → changes → approved → merged
Add custom aliases feature/custom-alias → main OPEN
Review is a conversation, not a gate — specific, kind, and timely feedback improves both the code and the people.
↳ Testing · OOP · Design Patterns
Reviewers apply the whole series: does the change have tests (Testing), does it respect encapsulation and SOLID (OOP), does it use the right pattern or force one where none belongs (Design Patterns)? Review is where that judgement gets shared rather than siloed in one head.
Code review is about finding fault and gatekeeping.
Its bigger value is shared understanding and better design. Framed as collaboration, review lifts the whole team; framed as gatekeeping, it breeds fear and rubber-stamps. Critique the code, support the author.
Approve quickly so you don't block anyone.
A rubber-stamp "LGTM" adds no value and lets defects through. But so does a review that sits for two days. The craft is timely and genuine — read it properly, soon, and distinguish real blockers from personal nitpicks.
TestingIntermediateModule 07

Testing in the lifecycle

Testing isn't a phase you reach after building — in a healthy lifecycle it runs throughout. This is the idea of shift-left: push testing as early as possible, so defects are caught when they're cheap rather than in production. Developers write automated tests alongside the code, those tests run on every push (Module 08), and only changes that keep them green move forward. "Done" isn't "the code compiles" — it's "the code works and is proven to, with tests."

The lifecycle uses the whole testing pyramid at different stages: a wide base of fast unit tests on every commit, a middle band of integration tests verifying components together, and a thin top of end-to-end tests against a staging environment before release. Quality is the whole team's job, not a separate department's — developers, reviewers, and QA all contribute to the same green bar.

↳ Testing in .NET · The Capstone
The Testing course is the deep dive — xUnit, mocks, the pyramid, TDD — and Snip already carries the suite: a property test on the encoder, a mocked service test, an integration test through WebApplicationFactory. This module is about where those tests run in the process: continuously, gating every change.
Testing is a phase after development is finished.
Bugs found late cost far more than bugs found early. Shift-left means writing and running tests as you build, so a regression is caught in seconds on your branch — not weeks later by a user. Testing is continuous, not terminal.
Quality is QA's responsibility.
When quality is "someone else's job," developers stop owning it and it erodes. Modern teams share it: automated tests written by developers, checked in review, and augmented — not replaced — by exploratory QA. The whole team keeps the bar green.
AutomationIntermediateModule 08

CI/CD

Continuous Integration is the practice of merging everyone's work frequently and verifying each change automatically: every push triggers a pipeline that builds the code and runs the tests, so integration problems surface in minutes instead of at a dreaded end-of-project merge. Continuous Delivery/Deployment extends that pipeline all the way to release — automatically shipping changes that pass every gate to staging, and often to production.

A pipeline is just a sequence of automated stages, each of which must pass before the next runs:

.github/workflows/ci.yml 
on: [push, pull_request]
jobs:
  build-test:
    steps:
      - uses: actions/checkout@v4
      - run: dotnet build
      - run: dotnet test           # red here blocks the merge
      - run: dotnet format --verify-no-changes

Run Snip's pipeline. Then break a test and watch the pipeline stop the bad change before it can reach production:

The pipeline
build → test → deploy, automatically
Every push runs the pipeline. A failure anywhere halts it — the broken change never reaches the next stage.
↳ Testing · Version control
CI is where the Testing suite earns its keep — it runs on every push to the branches from Module 05, automatically. The green checkmark on a pull request that reviewers rely on? That's the pipeline reporting back. Testing, version control, and review all converge here.
CI is just a server that compiles your code.
CI is the practice of integrating and verifying continuously; the server is only the tool. Its value is the discipline — small, frequent merges each proven green — that keeps the codebase always working instead of drifting into a broken mess.
Continuous delivery means every commit auto-deploys to production.
Continuous delivery keeps every change deployable and pushes it to staging automatically; a human can still approve the production step. Continuous deployment removes that gate. Both are valid — the choice is about risk tolerance, not correctness.
ReleaseAdvancedModule 09

Deployment & release

The pipeline can ship automatically — but shipping to everyone at once is how a small bug becomes a big outage. Deployment strategies exist to limit the blast radius. A rolling deploy replaces instances gradually; blue-green stands up the new version alongside the old and flips traffic in one switch (with instant rollback); canary sends a tiny slice of traffic to the new version first and only proceeds if it stays healthy.

Release v2 of Snip with each strategy — then mark v2 broken and watch how much damage each one lets through before rolling back:

limit the blast radius
rolling blue-green canary
traffic → v1 (old) vs v2 (new)
Progressive rollout means a bad release is caught while it affects a fraction of users — then rolled back automatically.
↳ Feature flags · CI/CD
Strategies pair with feature flags, which decouple deploy from release: code ships dark and is switched on for a few users, then everyone. That's the same [Flags] idea from the Bits course and Snip's LinkFlags, applied to rollout instead of storage.
Deploying means flipping one switch for all users.
An all-at-once deploy gives a bad change the maximum blast radius. Canary and rolling releases expose new code to a fraction first, so problems show up small and contained — the difference between a blip and an outage.
Rollback means manually redeploying the old version.
That's slow when minutes matter. Blue-green keeps the old version running for an instant switch-back, and feature flags turn a bad feature off without any deploy at all. Fast, automatic rollback is part of the release design, not an afterthought.
OperateExpertModule 10

Operate & maintain

Deployment isn't the finish line — it's the start of the longest phase. Software runs in production for years, and keeping it healthy is its own discipline. It begins with observability: logs, metrics, and traces that let you see what the system is actually doing. On top of that sit monitoring and alerting (know something's wrong before users tell you), on-call and incident response (fix it fast, then run a blameless postmortem so it can't recur), and targets like SLOs that define "healthy enough."

Alongside operations runs maintenance: fixing bugs, patching security holes, updating dependencies, and paying down technical debt before it compounds. And this is where the lifecycle closes its loop — what you learn from running the software in production becomes the input to the next round of planning. The redirect latency you're watching, the feature users keep requesting, the incident that revealed a design flaw: all of it flows back to Module 00, and the cycle begins again.

↳ Big O · The whole loop
What you monitor is exactly what the technical courses taught you to reason about — the O(1) redirect path from Big O, the index from SQL, the memory of the cache. Operations is those concepts observed under real load, feeding evidence back into design. The line becomes a circle.
Software is done when it ships.
Launch is roughly the midpoint of a system's life, not the end. Most of the total effort and cost goes into running, fixing, and evolving software after release. "Done" is a milestone in an ongoing loop, not a final state.
Technical debt is just messy code you can ignore.
Debt compounds: every shortcut makes the next change slower and riskier until the system ossifies. Like financial debt, a little can be a smart trade — but only if you track it and pay it down deliberately, not pretend it's free.
ExpertModule 11

Putting it together

Zoom out and the phases form one continuous flow. A single Snip feature — custom aliases — travels the whole lifecycle: it starts as a backlog item, gets built on a branch, passes review, goes green in CI/CD, is verified in staging, ships to production, and is watched there — and what's learned seeds the next item. Advance it through the loop:

A feature through the whole loop
backlog → prod → feedback → repeat
This is the lifecycle in motion. The frameworks below — Scrum, Kanban — are just different ways to organize this same flow.

Teams organize that flow with frameworks. Scrum batches work into fixed sprints with a backlog, a daily standup, and a review and retrospective at the end. Kanban drops the fixed cadence for continuous flow with limits on work-in-progress. And teams measure the health of the whole loop with the DORA metrics: how often they deploy, how long a change takes to reach production, how often changes fail, and how fast they recover. Fast, safe, frequent iteration is the goal — everything in this course serves it.

The lifecycle around the code

Every earlier course taught you to write good code; this one is everything around the code that turns it into software people can rely on. Requirements decide what to build, design shapes it, version control and review let a team build it together, CI/CD and testing prove it, deployment ships it safely, and operations keeps it alive and feeds the next round. Knowing the lifecycle is what separates someone who can write code from someone who can deliver software.

Ship it. Then do it again.

The lifecycle isn't a checklist you complete once — it's a loop you get better at spinning. Each turn, an idea becomes a small, working, tested slice of software that ships to real users and teaches you what to do next. The phases you just walked — plan, design, build, review, test, deploy, operate — aren't stops on a line; they're stations on a wheel that never really stops turning.

That's the shape of a career in this craft, too. You'll write the code — the fundamentals, the language, the frameworks, all of it — but delivering software is the loop that carries that code to the people who need it, again and again. Learn to love the loop, and everything else compounds.
